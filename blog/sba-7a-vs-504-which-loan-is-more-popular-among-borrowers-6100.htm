SBA 7a vs. 504: Which Loan Is More Popular Among Borrowers?

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

When it comes to financing a small business, two of the most talked-about loan programs are the SBA 7(a) loan and the SBA 504 loan. Both are backed by the U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA), but they serve different purposes and attract different types of borrowers.

In today’s economic climate—where inflation, supply chain disruptions, and rising interest rates dominate headlines—business owners must carefully choose the right financing option. So, which loan is more popular among borrowers? Let’s break it down.

Understanding SBA 7(a) and 504 Loans

Before comparing popularity, it’s essential to understand how these loans work.

What Is an SBA 7(a) Loan?

The SBA 7(a) loan is the SBA’s most flexible and widely used financing option. It provides funding for various business needs, including:
- Working capital
- Equipment purchases
- Debt refinancing
- Business acquisitions

Loan amounts can go up to $5 million, with repayment terms ranging from 7 to 25 years, depending on the use of funds.

What Is an SBA 504 Loan?

The SBA 504 loan, on the other hand, is specifically designed for long-term, fixed-asset financing. It’s ideal for:
- Purchasing commercial real estate
- Buying heavy machinery
- Constructing or renovating facilities

This loan structure involves three parties:
1. A borrower (contributing at least 10% equity)
2. A Certified Development Company (CDC) (providing 40% of the loan)
3. A private lender (covering the remaining 50%)

Maximum loan amounts vary but can reach $5.5 million (or more for certain projects).

Popularity Contest: SBA 7(a) vs. 504

Now, let’s examine which loan borrowers prefer—and why.

Volume of Loans Issued

Historically, the SBA 7(a) program has dominated in terms of loan volume. In fiscal year 2023, the SBA approved over 60,000 7(a) loans, totaling more than $27 billion. Meanwhile, the 504 program saw around 6,000 loans, worth approximately $9 billion.

Why the disparity?
- Flexibility: The 7(a) loan can be used for almost any business need, while the 504 is restricted to real estate and equipment.
- Easier access: Many small businesses don’t qualify for 504 loans because they don’t need large real estate purchases.

Borrower Demographics

Different industries and business stages favor one loan over the other.

Who Prefers SBA 7(a)?

  • Startups and younger businesses needing working capital
  • Service-based businesses (consulting firms, restaurants, retail stores)
  • Businesses acquiring another company

Who Prefers SBA 504?

  • Established businesses looking to expand facilities
  • Manufacturers and industrial companies buying heavy equipment
  • Medical practices purchasing office buildings

Interest Rates and Terms

Both loans offer competitive rates, but their structures differ.

  • SBA 7(a) loans typically have variable interest rates (tied to the prime rate) but can also be fixed.
  • SBA 504 loans offer long-term fixed rates, making them attractive for real estate investments.

In a high-interest-rate environment (like 2023-2024), some borrowers prefer the 504 loan’s stability, while others opt for 7(a) loans due to faster approval times.

Approval Process and Speed

  • SBA 7(a) loans are generally faster to secure (30-60 days).
  • SBA 504 loans take longer (60-90 days) due to the involvement of a CDC.

For businesses needing quick cash flow support, the 7(a) loan is often the go-to choice.

Current Trends Influencing Loan Popularity

Several macroeconomic factors are shaping borrower preferences in 2024.

Rising Commercial Real Estate Costs

With commercial property prices soaring, many businesses are turning to SBA 504 loans to lock in long-term financing before rates climb further.

Inflation and Supply Chain Pressures

Companies struggling with higher operational costs may prefer SBA 7(a) loans for working capital to cover payroll, inventory, and unexpected expenses.

Government Incentives and Policy Changes

Recent SBA rule changes, such as higher loan limits and reduced fees, have made both loans more attractive. However, the 7(a) program’s broader eligibility keeps it in higher demand.

Final Thoughts: Which Loan Wins?

While the SBA 7(a) loan is more popular overall due to its versatility, the 504 loan remains a powerhouse for real estate and equipment financing. The best choice depends on the borrower’s industry, financial needs, and growth plans.

For businesses needing quick, flexible funding, the 7(a) loan is the clear winner. But for those making major capital investments, the 504 loan’s fixed-rate advantage makes it a smarter long-term play.

In today’s uncertain economy, smart borrowers weigh their options carefully—and both SBA loans continue to play critical roles in small business growth.
